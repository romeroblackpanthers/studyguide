Civil Rights Groups - Who, what, when, where

ACLU (American Civil Liberties Union)

Who: Helen Keller, Roger Nash Baldwin, Jane Addams, and others
What: Works to protect the liberties granted under the Constitution. It helps to protect liberties such as freedom of expression, due process, and equality under the law by using legal measures.
When: January 19, 1920
Where: New York City

AERA (American Equal Rights Association)

Who: Susan B. Anthony, Elizabeth Cady Stanton, Lucy Stone, Frederick Douglass
What: Worked to secure equal voting rights regardless of race, color, or sex. After the failure of an amendment granting women the right to vote, the organization dissolved into other organizations.
When: 1866-1869
Where: New York and Kansas

AIM (American Indian Movement)

Who: Russell Means, Dennis Banks, Clyde Bellecourt
What: led protests advocating indigenous American interests, inspired cultural renewal, monitored police activities and coordinated employment programs in cities and in rural reservation communities across the United States.
When: 1968
Where: Minneapolis, MN

Black Panther Party [for Self Defense]

Who: Huey Newton, Bobby Seale
What: Militant group created to patrol African American neighborhoods to protect residents from acts of police brutality.
When: 1966-1982
Where: Oakland, CA

Brown Berets (Los Boinas Cafes)

Who: David Sanchez, other Mexican-American youths
What: Protested for farm workers' rights, educational reform, and anti-war activism. Part of the Chicano Movement (El Movimiento).
When: 1967
Where: Los Angeles, CA

Chicano Movement of the 1960s (El Movimiento)

Who: Cesar Chavez, Reies Lopez Tijerina, Hector Garcia, Rodolfo "Corky" Gonzales, Dolores Huerta, and other leading Latino activists.
What: To assist the equal treatment of Mexican Americans. Encompassed a broad list of issues—from restoration of land grants, to farm workers' rights, to enhanced education, to voting and political rights, as well as emerging awareness of collective history. Socially, the Chicano Movement addressed negative ethnic stereotypes of Mexicans in mass media and the American consciousness.
When: 1940s to 1970s
Where: Southwestern US
Supreme Court Cases: Mendez v. Westminster (1947), Hernandez v. Texas (1957)

CORE (Congress of Racial Equality)

Who: James Farmer, Bayard Rustin, George Houser, Bernice Fisher
What: to improve race relations and end discriminatory policies through direct action projects.
When: 1942
Where: Chicago

Defenders of State Sovereignty and Individual Liberties

Who: Richard Crawford
What: Political group that sought to preserve segregation in Virginia schools. Published its Plan for Virginia in 1955. Advocated for an amendment to state constitution to allow the creation of state tuition vouchers and to withdraw state funding for integrated schools. A ballot measure on January 9, 1956, passed by a vote of 304,154 to 146,164.
When: 1954-1967
Where: Petersburg, VA (founded), Richmond, VA (HQ)

El Congreso de Pueblos de Habla Español

Who: Luisa Moreno and other Latino labor and civil rights activists
What: Broad agenda of Latino unity that included immigrants living in the United States, also embraced the working class.
When: 1930s-1940s (declined due to FBI surveillance and suspicion of subversive activities)
Where: Los Angeles

Freedom Summer/Mississippi Summer Project

Who: Council of Federated Organizations (included SNCC , CORE, NAACP, and SCLC)
What: A volunteer program that registered African American voters across Mississippi in 1964. In 1962, 5.3% of African Americans were registered to vote in Mississippi.
When: 1964
Where: Mississippi
Impact: Resulted in the formation of the Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party (co-chaired by Fannie Lou Hamer), catalyzed the passage of Voting Rights Act of 1965, Murders of Chaney, Goodman, and Schwerner

Gay and Lesbian Alliance Against Defamation (GLAAD)

Who: Vito Russo, Jewelle Gomez, Lauren Hinds
What: To promote understanding, acceptance, and equality for the LGBT community. Protested against what it thought was defamatory and sensationalized AIDS coverage in the New York Post.
When: 1985
Where: New York City

Ku Klux Klan (KKK)

Who: Nathan Bedford Forrest and other ex-Confederate veterans
What: Supports white supremacy by using violence and intimidation.
When: 1866
Where: Southern US

John Birch Society

Who: Robert W. Welch Jr.
What: Self-described conservative advocacy group supporting anti-communism and limited government. It has been described as a racial right and far-right organization.
When: 1958
Where: Indianapolis, IN

League of United Latin American Citizens

Who: Pedro and Maria Hernandez
What: Oldest surviving Latino civil rights organization in the US. Sought to end ethnic discrimination against Hispanic veterans of World War 1. Also promoted capitalism and individualism, and a belief that Mexican-Americans could improve their socio-economic standing through assimilation and hard work.
When: 1929
Where: Corpus Christi, TX

League of Women Voters

Who: Carrie Chapman Catt
What: Formerly NAWSA. Founded during the 1920 convention of the National American Woman Suffrage Association, 6 months before 19th Amendment was ratified. Non-partisan group that encouraged women to vote and be political, as well as educating other citizens on issues and lobbying.
When: 1920
Where: Washington DC

National American Woman Suffrage Association (NAWSA)

Who: Susan B. Anthony, Carrie Chapman Catt, Alice Paul, Lucy Stone
What: Merger of two existing organizations, the National Woman Suffrage Association (NWSA) and the American Woman Suffrage Association (AWSA). It became the largest voluntary organization in the nation with a membership of 2 million and played a pivotal role in the passing of the 19th Amendment. It also cooperated actively with the WW1 war effort. Dissolved into the LWV in 1920.
When: 1890
Where: Washington DC

National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP)

Who: W.E.B. Du Bois, Ida B. Wells, and Mary White Ovington
What: Created to fight injustices such as discrimination and segregation using the court system. Published The Crisis magazine
When: 1909
Where: New York City

National Organization for Women

Who: Betty Friedan, Pauli Murray, and others
What: Founded at the Third National Conference of Commissions on the Status of Women (successor to the Presidential Commission on the Status of Women). Founded to challenge discrimination in employment, even after the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission and Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. Considered to be the "NAACP for women". Nowadays it supports many issues involving women's rights and equality.
When: 1966
Where: Washington DC

National Urban League

Who: Ruth Standish Baldwin, Dr. George Edmund Haynes, Eugene K. Jones
What: A service agency created to end racial segregation. It started as a combination of three separate organizations. Helped African Americans to move North and find jobs and housing. The mission of the NUL is "to enable African Americans to secure economic self-reliance, parity, power, and civil rights."
When: 1920
Where: New York City

Niagara Movement

Who: W.E.B. Du Bois, William Monroe Trotter
What: A black civil rights organization that called for full political and civil rights for African Americans now. Contrasted with Booker T. Washington's Atlanta Compromise that sought equality gradually. Eventually morphed into NAACP.
When: 1905-1910 (became NAACP)
Where: Fort Erie, Ontario

Olympic Project for Human Rights

Who: Harry Edwards, Tommie Smith, John Carlos, Muhammad Ali
What: The aim of the organization was to protest against racial segregation in the United States and elsewhere (like South Africa), and racism in sports in general. Advocated a boycott of the 1968 Summer Olympics in Mexico City. Although the boycott failed to materialize, both Tommie Smith and John Carlos (African American sprinters) wore OPHR patches during the 200m medal ceremony, and raised their hands in a black power salute during the US national anthem. Predominantly an African American organization, but supported by white athletes.
When: 1967-1968
Where: Worldwide

Project "C" (aka Birmingham Movement)

Who: Martin Luther King Jr., James Bevel, Fred Shuttlesworth, and the SCLC
What: Nonviolent measures of active CONFRONTATION were suggested, such as sit-ins, boycotts, and marches intended to provoke mass arrests. Model of nonviolent direct action that drew the world's attention to racial segregation in the South, ousted Eugene "Bull" Connor from his job, forced desegregation, and directly paved the way for the Civil Rights Act of 1964.
When: April 3 - May 10, 1963
Where: Birmingham, AL

Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC)

Who: Martin Luther King Jr., Ralph Abernathy, Bayard Rustin, Fred Shuttlesworth, Joseph Lowery
What: African American civil rights organization that uses nonviolent means to achieve equality. Advocacy of boycotts was seen as controversial; many black leaders believed segregation should be challenged in the courts and that direct action excited white resistance.
When: 1957
Where: Atlanta, GA

Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC)

Who: Ella Baker, Julian Bond, Diane Nash, Bernard Lafayette, Charles Sherrod
What: Founded by a group of African American college students. Staged many nonviolent events and actions like sit-ins, freedom rides, marches, and rallies. Renamed Student National Coordinating Committee in 1969 after they started using more violent measures to gain equality.
When: 1960
Where: North Carolina

United Farm Workers

Who: Cesar Chavez and Dolores Huerta (merger of AWOC and NFWA)
What: Protection of laborers, along with improving social and economic conditions, and to fight discrimination.
When: 1962
Where: Keene, California

Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA)

Who: Marcus Garvey
What: It was dedicated to racial pride, economic self-sufficiency, and the formation of an independent black nation in Africa.
When: 1914
Where: Jamaica and United States
