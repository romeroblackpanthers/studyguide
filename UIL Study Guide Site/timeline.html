Timeline

1848

At the Weslayan Chapel in Seneca Falls, NY, the first-ever women's rights convention begins with almost 200 women in attendance.

1865

Confederate veterans found the KKK as a secret fraternity that grew to a militant group to reverse Reconstruction Era policies that elevated the rights of the black population.

1865-1870

Reconstruction Amendments (13-15) are passed: ending slavery, granting citizenship, and extending voting rights.

1869

Wyoming is the first to give women the right to vote.

1877

The Jim Crow Era (named for a minstrel routine) begins with laws that enforce racial segregation in the South after the end of Reconstruction.

1896

On May 18th, SCOTUS releases a 7-1 decision in Plessy v. Ferguson ruling "separate but equal" is a constitutional justification for segregating public accomodations in states.

1909

The National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP) is founded in NYC.

1916

Jeannette Rankin, the first woman to hold federal office in the U.S., is elected to the U.S. House of Representatives by the state of Montana.

1920

Passed by Congress on June 4th, 1919, the 19th Amendment granting U.S. women the right to vote was ratified on August 18th.

1924

The Chicago based Society for Human Rights is chartered on December 10th as the earliest gay rights organization in the U.S.

1929

The League of United Latin American Citizens (LULAC) was founded on February 17th. The group organizes to become the largest and oldest Latino civil rights group.

1932

Hattie Wyatt Caraway became the first woman elected to serve a full team as a U.S. senator representing Arkansas.

1946

The President's Committee on Civil Rights was established by President Harry Truman. On July 26th, 1948, Truman put some of the suggestions into effect by signing executive orders 9980 and 9981.

1951

Donald Webster Cory publishes The Homosexual in America asserting that gay men and lesbians deserve civil rights.

1954

On May 17th, Earl Warren delivered the unanimous ruling in Brown v. Board of Education ruling segregation of public schools is a violation of the 14th Amendment.

1955

  • While visiting family in Mississippi from his home in Chicago, 14 year old Emmett Till was kidnapped and murdered for allegedly flirting with a white woman.


  • The Montgomery Bus Boycott starts four days after Rosa Parks is arrested for not giving up her seat. It lasted just over a year until December 20th, 1956 when the SCOTUS forced the city to integrate the bus system.

    • 1956

    The Declaration of Constitutional Principles (The Southern Manifesto) is written in Congress by 101 members of Southern state delegations in response to the Brown decision.

    1957

    The "Mob Rule Cannot Be Allowed to Override the Decisions of Our Courts" address by President Dwight D. Eisenhower is given in response to the Little Rock, AR opposition to integration.

    1958

    The SCOTUS ruled in One, Inc. v. Olsen that a magazine for the gay community was not obscene and the refusal to deliver it by the U.S. Postal Service was unconstitutional.

    1960

  • The first sit-ins at what would become a widespread movement start when young black students stage a sit-in at a segregated Woolworth's lunch counter in Greensboro, NC.


  • The FDA approves the pill as a contraceptive. Within five years it is the most popular form of contraceptive taken in the U.S.

    • 1961

  • Freedom Riders rode interstate buses into the South to challenge the non-enforcement of the SCOTUS orders to desegregate bus lines and stations


  • The President's Committee on the Status of Women is established on December 14th by U.S. President John F. Kennedy to investigate questions regarding women's equality and is chaired by Eleanor Roosevelt.

    • 1962

  • The United Farm Workers Organizing Committee led by Cesar Chavez is started in Delano, CA and leads to the United Farm Workers Association.


  • The Ole Miss Riot, or Battle of Oxford, was fought on September 30th as segregationists protested the enrollment of James Meredith and attacked federal law enforcement.

    • 1963

  • The Feminine Mystique written by Betty Friedan is credited with beginning the second-wave feminism and was published on February 19th.


  • Gov. George Wallace stands in the door at the University of Alabama on June 11th as he tried to stop Vivian Malone and James Hood from registering. In response, JFK federalized the Alabama National Guard.


  • In the early morning hours of June 12th, Byron De La Beckwith shot NAACP leader Medgar Evers in his driveway as he arrived home.


  • The March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom occurred in August with nearly 1/4 million people in front of the Lincoln Memorial in Washington, D.C. Martin Luther King's now iconic "I Have A Dream" speech highlighted the event.



  • A bomb exploded before Sunday morning services at the 16th Street Baptist Church in Birmingham, AL killing four young girls.

    • 1964

  • The 24th Amendment banning poll taxes is ratified on January 23rd after proposed the year before by Congress.


  • The MFDP was founded on April 26th as part of a voter registration project to run candidates for the Senate and Congressional elections.


  • The Civil Rights Act of 1964 is passed prohibiting segregation in public places and banned employment discrimination on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin.


  • The Freedom Summer, or the Mississippi Summer Project, is launched to register voters in Mississippi. On June 21st, James Chaney, Andrew Goodman, and Michael Schwerner were arrested in Neshoba County and went missing shortly thereafter. They were found dead and buried in an earthen dam.



  • The Harlem riots occurred between July 16th-22nd after James Powell was shot and killed by police in front of his friends. In total, 4,000 people participated in the riots which led to one dead rioter, 118 injured, and 465 arrested.

    • 1965

  • In New York City, black nationalist and religious leader Malcolm X is assassinated by rival Black Muslims during a speech to his Organization of Afro-American Unity.


  • A March 7th protest of the killing of Jimmie Lee Jackson was planned from Selma to Montgomery, AL. Led by John Lewis, the group of about 600 was attacked by a mix of law enforcement while kneeling near the Edmund Pettus Bridge. The brutal attack was televised around the world.



  • The Selma to Montgomery, AL voting rights protest marched the 54-mile route and Martin Luther King, Jr.'s participation in it raised awareness of the difficulties faced by black voters and the need for a national Voting Rights Act.


  • The Voting Rights Act of 1965 is signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson to overcome legal barriers to rights granted by the 15th Amendment.

    • 1966

  • From March-April, Cesar Chavez and the National Farm Workers Association march from Delano to the California state capitol in Sacramento.


  • The National Organization for Women (NOW) is founded in Washington, D.C. on June 30th.


  • The Black Panthers was founded in Oakland, California by Huey Newton and Bobby Seale to challenge police brutality.


  • Edward William Brooke III becomes the first black American popularly elected to the U.S. Senate representing Massachusetts.

    • 1967

  • LBJ appoints U.S. Court of Appeals Judge Thurgood Marshall on August 30th, and after a vote of 69-11, he is confirmed as the first black member of the SCOTUS.

    • 1968

  • Cesar Chavez begins a 25-day fast in response to violent repression of farm workers.


  • MLK is assassinated on April 4th in Memphis, Tennessee by James Earl Ray as he stood on his hotel's balcony. Riots spread throughout black neighborhoods and cities. One week later, LBJ signs the Civil Rights Act of 1968 into law prohibiting discrimination in renting and selling property.



  • The Mexican American Legal Defense and Education Fund begins its work to protect the civil rights of Mexican Americans.


  • The American Indian Movement (AIM) is founded in July in Minneapolis, Minnesota to address American Indian Sovereignty, incidents of police harassment, and racism against Native Americans.

    • 1969

  • On June 28th, New York City police raided the Stonewall Inn, a gay nightclub. It led to six days of protests against law enforcement in Greenwich Village. The incident helped start a larger gay rights movement in the U.S.


  • From November 20th, 1969-June 11th, 1971, Native Americans took over and held Alcatraz Island as Indians of All Tribes (IAT). It ended when IAT was removed by the federal government.

    • 1970

  • The Women's Strike for Equality took place in the U.S. on August 26th. It celebrated the 50th anniversary of the 19th Amendment and was sponsored by the National Organization of Women (NOW). It was the largest gathering of women in U.S. history at that point.


    • 1972

  • On March 22nd, the Equal Rights Amendment (ERA) is passed in the Senate and sent to the states for ratification after being proposed by the National Woman's political party in 1923.


  • On June 23rd, Title IX of the Education Amendments is signed into law by Richard Nixon prohibiting sex discrimination in any educational program receiving federal funds.

    • 1973

  • On the Pine Ridge Reservation in South Dakota, some 200 Sioux Native Americans, led by members of the American Indian Movement (AIM), occupied Wounded Knee to protest conditions. Two were killed by the FBI before the protestors surrendered after 71 days.


    • 1974

  • Ella Grasso became the first woman elected as a state governor (Connecticut) without having been the spouse of widow of a former governor.

    • 1975

  • The United Nations began celebrating International Women's Day in the International Women's Year. Two years later they moved to have member states highlight women's efforts on March 8th of each year.


  • U.S. Congress expands the U.S. Voting Rights Act to require language assistance at polling stations to help deal with reported discrimination.

    • 1977

  • On November 18th-21st, about 20,000 people took part in the National Women's Conference in Houston to create a "Plan of Action" to be presented to the Carter administration and Congress. A counter rally under Phyllis Schafly was held across town.


    • 1978

  • The Moscone-Milk assassinations in the San Francisco City Hall by former Supervisor Dan White took place on November 27th. White was subsequently convicted of voluntary manslaughter, rather than first-degree murder, leading to the "White Night riots."


    • 1982

  • By the year of expieration for the ERA, 35 of the required 38 states had ratified it.

    • 1985

  • The Gay & Lesbian Alliance Against Defamation (GLAAD) is founded in NYC to protest against the New York Post's derogatory coverage of gay rights issues and AIDS.

    • 1987

  • Fifty thousand gay rights activists marched on Washington to demand that the Reagan administration address the growing AIDS epidemic.

    • 1990

  • The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) was signed into law by President George H.W. Bush on July 26th.

    • 1992

  • In Los Angeles, four police officers that had been caught beating Rodney King were acquitted of any wrongdoing. In response, riots in south-central LA lasted over three days and killed more than 60 people, injured almost 2,000 people, and caused nearly $1 billion in damage.


    • 1997

  • California's Prop. 187 was ruled unconstitutional in U.S. District Court three years after it was passed.

    • 2000

  • On April 26th, the first civil unions between same-sex couples became legal in the state of Vermont.

    • 2003

  • Latinos surpass blacks to become the nation's largest racial minority group.

    • 2009

  • The inauguration of Barack Obama as the 44th president of the U.S. took place on January 20th marking the first time a citizen of any minority group became president.


  • The Hate Crimes Prevention Act, or the Matthew Shepard Act, was signed into law by President Obama on October 28th, 2009 over 10 years after Matthew Shepard was singled out by his attackers because he was gay. He was tortured and left to die.


    • 2010

  • Gay Americans are allowed to serve openly in the armed forces after the National Defense Authorization Bill repeals "Don't Ask, Don't Tell."

    • 2014

  • July 17th, Eric Garner died in Staten Island after a NYPD officer put him in a headlock while arresting him for selling loose cigarettes. "I can't breathe" protests against excessive force and police brutality spread.


  • Unrest in Ferguson, Missouri began the day after the fatal shooting of Michael Brown by white police officer Darren Wilson on August 9th leading to a national debate about the militarization of police and the use of force. Protests and riots broke out again in November after a grand jury failed to indict Wilson.


    • 2016

  • Colin Kaepernick begins sitting for the national anthem during the 49ers preseason games to protest oppression of people of color and ongoing issues with police brutality.

    • 2017

  • The Women's March on January 21st took place to advocate policies regarding women's rights. It occurred directly after the inauguration of Donald Trump as president of the U.S. and was the largest single-day protest in U.S. history involving 3-5 million people.


  • The "Me Too" movement spreads virally in October 2017 as a hashtag used on social media to spread awareness of sexual assault and harassment following allegations against Harvey Weinstein.
